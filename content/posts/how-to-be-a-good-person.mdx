Be a good person. It's a pretty simple thing to understand, but we aren't ever taught the reason why, or even how, to be a good person. Take the trolley problem: a lot of people would say we should save those three people and sacrifice the one person. How about if that one person was your mother? Father? Then what is good? Utilitarians would say utility is equal for all people, so the person is irrelevant, while a host of other theories would say that saving one person is selfish, but because it is your parent, you have some moral obligation to that person. I believe that both sides are wrong, in fact. I believe that this is the one demonstration of where utilitarianism collapses.

Let's take a commonly agreed-upon definition: we ought to maximize society's best interests. This is posited as a "global truth/axiom."

But why?

To answer this question, we need to dig into the most fundamental question: what is the "purpose" of life? I believe the true answer is to survive and reproduce. You can disagree, but this is the most fundamental idea behind evolutionary biology. If you disagree again, feel free to message me or email me. I'm always open to disagreement, but I do believe disagreements at this level are not really important to the discussion at hand.

Now that we have established a why/purpose, let's consider:

## Why do we socialize?

Why do we, as humans, choose to be social over a lone hunter like a tiger? I think it's pretty simple: because cooperation is just an optimal way to survive and reproduce.

But there's a problem.

Remember how I said that we ought to maximize society's best interests always? What if that comes at a cost to the self?

Then we have logically deduced that we simply maximize our personal utility by maximizing societal utility.

## Back to the trolley problem

Under my definition of "utility," what we should be considering is actually this: does the personal trauma of killing a loved one outweigh the societal benefit to yourself? See, that's the question at hand. It's not a moral question, but actually a cost-benefit analysis question.

I believe that to be a good person, like a genuinely good person, it's about how you make your decisions. I believe the reason why a lot of people are considered "bad" is because they choose not to think about their decisions in a way that is truly rational. I think the greatest form of empathy comes from a consideration of all options and trying to make the best decision.

Let's say you have a ~35% chance, and let's assume our opponents have AA and AK, and the board comes K 8 2 7, and your hand is 5–6. If both your opponents jam the flop, you need to call the jam on the turn because of pot odds.

## Putting it together

Let me try to put this together in a coherent way. See, I don't think you should be selfish. I believe that you should view "good" and "bad" not as a binary, but actually as a distribution. You need to try to make EV decisions on that distribution, but sometimes you need to make -EV decisions and take on variance, like not jumping in front of a fire to save three random people.
